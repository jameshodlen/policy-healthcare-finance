Transparency Range

Federal law requires every hospital to publish a machine-readable file listing what it charges every insurer for every service. Most hospitals comply. Some comply fully — disclosing negotiated rates for dozens of payers across thousands of procedures. Others publish files that technically exist but contain almost nothing.

This platform profiles every chargemaster file we can access, scoring each hospital on what it actually discloses. The data is public. The contradictions are structural.

Anchor Findings

  1. UChicago Medical Center discloses payer rates for 2.9% of items — exclusively DRG bundles. The other 97% of services show only list prices. It's the least transparent academic medical center in the three-state dataset.
  2. HealthPartners Regions Hospital in St. Paul scores 6.9% — the lowest non-zero transparency in 99 hospitals. As both an insurer and a hospital, its transfer pricing is structurally opaque.
  3. Mayo Clinic Rochester discloses rates for 1,102 payer/plan combinations — ten times more than any other hospital. The file is an analytical gold mine.
  4. ThedaCare achieves 100% transparency at all 9 Wisconsin facilities with up to 59 payers. Full disclosure is not a technical challenge — it's a choice.
  5. At UW Health, Anthem PPO pays an estimated $4,237 for a brain MRI while Medicare pays $357 — an 11.9:1 commercial-to-Medicare ratio for the same scan at the same hospital.
  6. At Meriter Hospital in Madison, the same knee replacement (DRG 470) costs Anthem PPO $46,834 but Medicare Advantage pays $14,825 — a 3.2x spread within one building.
  7. Froedtert Hospital — Milwaukee's flagship Level I Trauma Center — discloses rates for only 6 payers. A 25-bed critical access hospital in rural Boscobel discloses more.

What This Platform Tracks

Every number on this platform is programmatically extracted from the CMS-mandated machine-readable files that hospitals publish on their websites. We download each file, parse its structure, and compute transparency metrics — the percentage of items with payer-specific negotiated rates, the number of payers disclosed, cash discount formulas, and schema compliance.

We do not editorialize the data. We trace the money: how much each payer negotiates, how that compares to Medicare, and what the hospital charges a patient who pays cash. The files are public. The patterns are structural. The contradictions speak for themselves.
